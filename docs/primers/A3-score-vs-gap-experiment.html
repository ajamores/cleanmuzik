T-008 · The auto-accept experiment

Teaching the machine
when to trust itself.

Every song you download hits one gate: file it silently, or stop and ask you. This is the day we measured exactly how sure "sure enough" should be — on your real music.

Where this fits

One gate, one decision

When CleanMuzik pulls a song off YouTube, it doesn't just guess a name from the title — titles lie. It listens to the actual sound and asks a fingerprint service (AcoustID) one question: which exact recording is this?

If the answer comes back certain, the song is tagged and filed with no clicks from you. If it's shaky, the song is parked in a review queue for you to pick. The whole art is drawing that line in the right place — too loose and it files wrong songs; too strict and it bothers you constantly.

What we were actually setting

A single dial with two settings — how high the confidence has to be, and how clearly the winner must beat the runner-up. The old values were an educated guess written before we had any data. Today we replaced the guess with measurement.

The two numbers that decide

Score and Gap

Every fingerprint lookup comes back with a ranked list of possible recordings. We only care about two things from that list.

Number one · Score

How sure is the top guess?

A confidence from 0 to 1 for the single best-matching recording. 0.99 means "this is almost certainly that exact recording." 0.30 means "vaguely similar, wouldn't bet on it."

Like a face-unlock saying "98% sure that's you." High enough, and the phone opens without a passcode.
Number two · Gap

How clearly did it win?

The distance between the top guess and the best different song beneath it. A big gap means one clear winner. A tiny gap means two candidates are neck-and-neck — a coin-flip we shouldn't auto-decide.

Like an election: 98%-to-2% is a mandate. 51%-to-49% is a recount — even if the leader's "score" is high.

Why both? A high score alone can still be a near-tie between two versions. The gap is meant to catch that ambiguity and send it to you. That was the theory. Then the data said something more interesting.

Look at the shape. The recognised songs don't scatter — they slam into the far-right wall (scores 0.955–0.995) or they sit at rock bottom (no match at all). There's almost nothing in the mushy middle. The fingerprint is either sure or silent. That single fact is what makes a clean line possible.

The finding that changed the dial

When "clear winner" backfires

The amber dot is Kanye's "Through The Wire." Its score was 0.987 — dead certain. But its gap was 0.010, almost nothing. By the old rule, a gap that small means "too close to call — park it." So a song we're certain about would have been sent to you for review.

Why so close? We looked at what the "runner-up" actually was:

The runner-up was the same song

Winner  → Through the Wire — Kanye West · 0.987
Runner-up → Through the Wire — Kanye West · 0.977

The fingerprint database simply holds two copies of the same song — a re-release under a different catalogue entry. They're not rivals. They're the same track twice.

The lesson: the Gap rule was built to catch two different songs fighting for the top spot. But fingerprints don't produce that fight — a different song never scores 0.97 against your audio. A high runner-up almost always means "the same song, listed twice." So the Gap penalty mostly punishes the duplicates, which are exactly the matches we're most sure of. The number meant to add safety was quietly costing us certainty.

Your question: what about the two it didn't know?

When the fingerprint finds nothing

You asked a sharp question: those two "no match" songs — did the fingerprint at least guess? Could it say "maybe this, maybe that, maybe the other"? We pulled the raw response. The honest answer:

No guesses. Just silence.

Bacc 2 Harlem → status: ok · results returned: 0

AcoustID is not a "closest guess" engine. It doesn't hand back a shortlist of maybes. It either recognises the exact acoustic fingerprint — and gives you the artist, title and releases with it — or it returns an empty set. For these two, the database had never seen that sound. Nothing to offer.

So where do the "maybe this, maybe that" suggestions come from? A completely separate path. This is the heart of the review-queue design:

Path 1 · The fingerprint

Certainty, or nothing

  • Listens to the actual audio.
  • Match → exact recording, artist and all. Files itself.
  • No match → total silence. No hints.
Path 2 · The title search

The "maybe this, maybe that" list

  • Reads the song's title and searches MusicBrainz by name.
  • Returns a shortlist of candidates — this exactly is your "maybe this / this / this."
  • Populates the review queue for you to pick from.

Here's why those two came up empty on both paths: they had no fingerprint match and no tags — the filename "Bacc 2 Harlem" was all there was to search, and MusicBrainz didn't know it either. But a fresh YouTube rip is different: it arrives carrying its title (Bad Bunny - Me Fui de Vacaciones), so even when the fingerprint whiffs, the title search still fills your shortlist. That is why good titles matter, and why the review panel is built around them.

A field note worth keeping

The private-playlist trap

A public or unlisted playlist works. A private one fails — silently.

The downloader can't resolve a link to a private playlist. And it won't tell you that's the reason — it just throws a generic "invalid URL" and leaves you guessing. If a playlist ever refuses to load, the first thing to check is its visibility setting: switch it to Unlisted and the same link works instantly. An hour lost to this once is an hour never lost again — so it's written down.

The title lied; the sound didn't

Your playlist had a track titled only "No One Knows" — that could be Queens of the Stone Age or a dozen others. The fingerprint ignored the name and identified the actual audio as Brent Faiyaz at 0.981. This is the entire point of fingerprint-first: names are guesses, sound is proof.

Where coverage thins

Every genre landed — Brazilian, Latin, US and UK R&B — except the Amapiano track, which the database had simply never catalogued. That's not a bug; it's the honest edge of what a fingerprint can know, and exactly why the review queue exists to catch it.

An honest footnote on this run

Five more playlist songs aren't in this table — not because they failed to match, but because the free fingerprint tier rate-limited us into the ground. Nearly every lookup had to be retried, and five never got an answer at all. That throttling is its own finding: the everyday app will need patient retries and, ideally, its own dedicated fingerprint key — noted for the next ticket.

Where the dial lands

The tuned settings

From the data, not a guess. One setting held; one was rethought.

Score must clear
0.90

Held. Every correct match scored 0.955 or higher; every non-match sat at 0.000. A clean, wide no-man's-land between them — 0.90 sits comfortably in the middle with room to spare.

Gap must clear
0.10 → 0.0

Switched off. A gap requirement only ever parked certain matches whenever the database lists a song twice ("Through The Wire") — across all 25 songs it never once caught a real mistake. So the gap is set to zero: the score decides on its own. It stays in the code as a dial we can turn back on if real-world use ever proves it's needed.

The point of the experiment wasn't to pick two numbers. It was to stop guessing them — to let your own music, tags and all (or no tags at all), tell us where the line belongs.
