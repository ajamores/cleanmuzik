The way it used to go

My old download routine

For years, getting a song meant the same tired dance. Every step added a little more chaos, and nothing ever cleaned up after itself.

  1. Find the song on YouTube — sometimes on my phone, sometimes my laptop, whichever was closest.

  2. Google "youtube to mp3" and click whatever converter came up first — a different random site almost every time.

  3. Download the MP3. It crammed the whole video title into the title field and left everything else — artist, album, year, genre, cover art — completely blank.

  4. Do it from my phone next time, through a different site, and get a differently-broken file for the same kind of song.

  5. Drop it all into whatever folder I'd made that month — April2024MUsic, July music — buried in OneDrive, and lose track.

  6. End up with hundreds of files, no two tagged the same way. Nothing could sort, search, or make a playlist from it.

Why it drove me up the wall

The six things that broke

The root of it was inconsistency. Different converter each time, phone one day and computer the next — so every single download was broken in its own way, and none of them agreed.

✕

Just a title, nothing else

The Googled converters shoved the whole video title into one field and left artist, album, year and genre completely blank.

⇄

Every download different

A different random site each time, phone vs computer — so no two files were tagged the same way. Pure chaos.

▢

A wall of grey squares

Zero cover art, ever. Scrolling my library was a joyless grid of blank placeholders.

♪

No genres, no structure

Couldn't make a "hip-hop" or "chill" mix, or filter by decade. The data to do it simply wasn't there.

⧉

Duplicates across devices

The same song grabbed on my phone and laptop, under two different junk names. No way to spot them.

⁂

Scattered & cloud-bloated

Fifteen "month" folders eating my OneDrive storage and syncing over and over. No structure at all.

When it isn't sure, it asks — it never guesses

About 88% of the time it's certain and files the song silently. When a rip is genuinely ambiguous — a live version, a remix, a mystery title — it doesn't mislabel it. It sets that one aside in a review queue and shows me a few candidates to pick from. Certainty or a question, never a wrong guess.

The clever bit

It knows the song by its sound, not its name.

This is my favourite part. The filename lies constantly — "Official Video", "y2mate", or just plain wrong. So CleanMuzik ignores the name entirely. It takes an acoustic fingerprint of the actual audio — like Shazam — and asks a giant open database "which exact recording is this?"

The answer comes back with the real title, artist, album and year attached. Names are a guess. Sound is proof.

The cast of characters

The tools that solve it

I didn't hand-build any of the hard parts — the smart move was wiring together brilliant free tools that each already do one thing incredibly well. Here's the whole crew, in plain terms.

yt-dlp
The grabber

Pulls the raw audio straight off a YouTube link. Handles single songs and whole playlists.

ffmpeg
The converter

Turns whatever got grabbed into one clean MP3 320 — the same high quality, every single time.

beets
The librarian

The brain of the whole thing. Identifies, tags, and files each track. Everything below plugs into it.

AcoustID + Chromaprint
The ear

Listens to the actual sound and says exactly which recording it is — even when the filename is nonsense.

MusicBrainz
The encyclopedia

A giant open music database with the real title, artist, album, and year for millions of songs.

Last.fm
The genre tagger

Fills in what kind of music each track is, so genre and decade playlists actually work.

Cover Art Archive
The album art

Finds the real, high-resolution album cover and bakes it right into the file.

LRCLib
The lyrics

Grabs synced lyrics so the words scroll in time while the song plays.

FastAPI
The engine room

The Python backend that runs the assembly line and keeps everything moving in order.

React
The face

The slick screen where I paste a link and watch each song animate through the pipeline live.

Jellyfin
The home shelf

My own private Spotify — it stores, streams, and plays the finished library on any device.

Tailscale
The private tunnel

Lets my phone reach the music at home from anywhere — without ever putting it on the open internet.

How it all connects

Where the music actually lives

Here's the shape of the whole thing. The big idea: my music only ever lives on my own machine at home. My devices talk only to that server — the server is the one that reaches out to the internet to fetch and identify songs. When I stream on my phone, it's not "the cloud"; it's a private tunnel reaching back to files that never left the house.

your devices ⇄ your server (private) server → internet (fetch & identify) home — the music never leaves

Phase 0 (now): it's just my laptop talking to itself over localhost. Phase 1 (later): the same server moves to a dedicated always-on PC, and my phone reaches it through a private Tailscale tunnel from anywhere — flip the toggle above to see it.
