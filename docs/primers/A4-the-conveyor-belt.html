Three decisions worth understanding — the real thing, then in plain words

How the belt is built.

Decision 01 · the queue

One worker, one song at a time

▸ what we actually built

A single background thread drains a queue. POST /api/jobs only enqueues the song and answers instantly with a job_id; the belt runs off the web request, never on it. Two near-simultaneous pastes still run strictly one-after-another. This is ADR-001 — do not parallelise the pipeline.

◆ in plain words

There's one worker, not a crowd. Paste two links and the second politely waits its turn.

A kitchen with one chef who cooks each dish start-to-finish before the next. Slower than a brigade — but the download and “name that tune” services get grumpy and start refusing you if you rush them all at once. One-at-a-time keeps them friendly.

Decision 02 · state

Two memories — a logbook and a sticky note

▸ what we actually built

The durable status (queued → running → done | review | error) lives in SQLite and survives a restart. The live per-stage detail — which station the song is at right now — lives in an in-memory registry. Asking GET /api/jobs/{id} overlays the two. The live stream of updates itself is the next job (T-013); this ticket lays the track it runs on.

◆ in plain words

A permanent logbook and a temporary sticky note.

The logbook records the basics — done, waiting, or failed — in ink, so a restart never loses them. The sticky note tracks the play-by-play (“converting now”) and is fine to lose on restart, because if the app restarts, nothing is mid-song anyway.

Decision 03 · cleanup

When to shred the working copy

▸ what we actually built

Each song gets one scratch folder. On a landed or failed outcome the folder is deleted. On a parked outcome it is kept — because that staging file is the copy the review queue will resolve. The rule comes straight from the identify stage's own contract, so the two never disagree.

◆ in plain words

Bin the rough draft — unless it's the one you still have to decide on.

A working copy rides along in a scratch tray. If the song files cleanly, or fails outright, the scratch copy is shredded. But if it's set aside for your review, that copy is the very thing you'll be looking at — so it's kept until you say.
