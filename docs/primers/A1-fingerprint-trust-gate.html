The problem · why we can't use beets' own confidence

beets grades an album. We only ever have a single track.

beets was built to identify a whole album — it corroborates a match against the track listing, the running order, the year, the album title. A YouTube rip has none of that. It's a lone song with no album around it — a singleton. So beets' own confidence score can never climb high enough, even when the match is correct.

beets' match distance — lower is better

0.00 — perfect0.04 — “strong” bar0.30 — no match
The green zone is “strong” — where beets auto-accepts. It ends at 0.04. A correct singleton match sits at ≈ 0.11 and never moves left. Measured, 3/3 in the spike.

That 0.11 is a structural floor, not bad luck. With no album to corroborate, the distance simply can't fall to 0.04. If we waited for “strong,” every song would go to review — the auto-accept rate would be zero. We measured exactly that: 0 of 3.

The move · ADR-006

Stop grading tidiness. Trust identity.

Here's the thing the distance score hides: the fingerprint already knows the answer. The acoustic fingerprint (chroma → fpcalc → AcoustID) listens to the actual audio and returns the correct recording with a high match score — regardless of how messy the tags are. The identity is certain even when the tidiness score is mediocre.

So we move the gate. It no longer asks “is this tidy enough?” (the distance score, which we throw out). It asks “is one recording clearly winning the fingerprint match?” — and treats a dominant fingerprint as ground truth. Everything else goes to you. That's the whole idea of T-007 in one sentence.

The decision · move the dials

“Dominant” is two numbers, not a feeling.

A match is trusted only when both hold: the top candidate's fingerprint score is high, and there's a clear gap down to the runner-up. High score alone isn't enough — two near-identical scores mean the machine can't tell two recordings apart, and that's exactly when it should ask you. Drag the dials.

Wait — what's the “runner-up”?

The fingerprint doesn't come back with one answer. AcoustID returns a ranked list of candidate recordings, each with its own score — and the runner-up is simply the second name on that list. There's more than one because a single song genuinely exists as many distinct recordings in MusicBrainz, and they can sound nearly identical:

  • the album version vs. the single edit vs. the 2011 remaster;
  • the same track on its original album and on three “Greatest Hits” compilations — each a separate database entry;
  • a live, acoustic, or remix cut.

So the gate never doubts the song — the fingerprint nails that. It's deciding which pressing of it. A wide gap means one recording is the clear winner; a tiny gap means two pressings are effectively tied, and choosing blind could file the remaster when you wanted the original. That is the case it hands to you.

The mechanism · how it hooks into beets

We don't call the tagger. We sit inside beets' import and answer one question.

beets runs the import as a session that normally prompts a human at the choice point. We subclass that session and answer the prompt in code — reading the ranked candidates, applying our two-number test, and either accepting the match or returning SKIP to leave the disk untouched and park the row. Every plugin (art, genre, lyrics) still runs as a stage; we never bypass them.

server/app/import_seam.py  ·  the shape (not final)
# A YouTube song is always a singleton → override choose_item, not choose_match.
class TrustFingerprint(ImportSession):
    def choose_item(self, task):
        cands = task.candidates            # ranked: TrackMatch(distance, TrackInfo)
        top, runner = cands[0], cands[1:2]

        score = fingerprint_score(top)     # ← the AcoustID score, NOT task.rec
        gap   = score - fingerprint_score(runner)

        if score >= 0.90 and gap >= 0.10:
            return top                 # dominant → apply tags, land the file

        park_to_review(task, cands)        # store candidate IDs + task.rec in SQLite
        return Action.SKIP          # weak → touch nothing, wait for the owner

Two rules the reviewer enforces: we never lower beets' global strong_rec_thresh to force acceptance — that would also wave through genuinely bad tag matches. And the whole thing runs on a worker thread, off the web server's event loop (that plumbing is T-012).

The two exits · what you actually see

Same gate, two doors.

● Door 1 — dominant → land

The card fills in and turns green.

  • Matched title, artist, album, cover art appear.
  • beets tags the file, embeds art + lyrics, adds genre.
  • Filed as MP3 320 under CleanMuzik/Artist/Album/.
  • Jellyfin is nudged to scan; it shows up in seconds.
  • Your click count: zero.
● Door 2 — weak → review

The card flips to “Needs review.”

  • The candidate list is shown — each with title, artist, album, year, thumbnail.
  • Plus the normalized query we searched (that's what T-006 built).
  • You accept the top, pick an alternate, or reject.
  • Resolving re-runs the import with your pick and lands it.
  • Survives a restart — the queue is on disk (SQLite).

Per the spike's measurement, Door 2 is the common path, not the rare exception the old PRD assumed. That's not a flaw — it's the point. The review queue is the product. T-007 just makes sure the easy ones never bother you.

Before we write it · the honest open questions

What this walkthrough is glossing over.

The concept is settled. Two build-time unknowns are not — and I'd rather name them now than discover them mid-diff.

The same three, in plain words

1 · The number problem — the real risk. Two different “confidence” numbers exist here, and it's easy to grab the wrong one. One measures how well the text tags line up — title, artist — and that's the 0.11 the spike saw. The other measures how well the actual audio matches, and that's the one our whole plan trusts. The catch: beets buries that second number somewhere inside the fingerprint plugin's result, and we haven't confirmed yet how to reach it. So step one when building is simply: go find that number. If it turns out to be locked away, we rework the plan around what we can read.

2 · The two dials are guesses — later, T-008. 0.90 and 0.10 are just a sensible starting point, not gospel. Once the gate works, we push a real batch of your songs through it, watch where the right and wrong matches actually land, and set the numbers from evidence instead of a hunch.

3 · Duplicates move in next door — later, T-009. The same machinery will also catch re-pasting a song you already own — keep the better copy, or ask you when they're too close to call. Same review screen, separate ticket.

Open — the real risk

Where does the fingerprint score live? The spike measured beets' tag distance (0.11). ADR-006 wants the AcoustID score behind a candidate — a different number. First thing we prove when building: how to read it off the chroma plugin's result. If it's awkward to reach, the seam design bends around it.

Deferred — T-008

The two thresholds are guesses. 0.90 / 0.10 are a starting point. T-008 runs a real batch of your songs through this gate, sees where correct and wrong matches actually fall, sets the numbers, and records the measured auto-accept rate back into ADR-006.

Adjacent — T-009

The same session handles duplicates. A second override, resolve_duplicate, catches re-pasting a song you already own — keep the better copy, or ask you if they're too close to call. Same review UI. Its own ticket.
