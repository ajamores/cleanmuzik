CleanMuzik · Networking Primer 01

VPN vs VPS —
where your music actually lives

Two acronyms one letter apart, routinely mixed up — and they do almost opposite jobs. Getting this straight explains the single biggest hosting decision in the whole project: why your library lives at home, and never in the cloud.

VPS = a computer you rent, somewhere else · VPN = a private tunnel to a computer you own

The one-letter trap

Same three letters, opposite ideas

They sound like cousins. They're not. One is a place to put a computer; the other is a private road to reach one. Confusing them is like confusing a storage unit with the key to your own front door — both involve "getting to your stuff," but one holds it somewhere else and one just lets you in.

VPS · Virtual Private Server

A computer you rent, in someone else's building

A slice of a machine in a data centre — a company's warehouse full of servers, in some city that isn't yours. You pay monthly; whatever you put on it lives there, on their hardware.

VPN · Virtual Private Network

A private tunnel to a computer you already own

An encrypted road connecting your devices, so your phone can reach your home PC as if it were sitting right next to it — from anywhere — without opening that PC to the public internet. It moves nothing to the cloud. It just lets you in.

The two analogies

Picture it

A VPS is renting a locker in a storage facility across town. Your things live there now. You drive over (or log in) to reach them. Stop paying and you lose the locker.

A VPN is a secret private hallway that runs from wherever you are straight into your own house. Your things never left home — the hallway just means distance stops mattering. Nobody else can see or use the hallway.

The decision this drives

Which does CleanMuzik use?

A VPN. Never a VPS.

Your music library has to live where Jellyfin runs — beets writes the files, Jellyfin reads the same files off the same disk. Put that on a VPS and you've moved your entire music collection onto a rented computer in the cloud: monthly cost, someone else's hardware, your library not really yours. So the library stays home, on hardware you own — and a VPN (Tailscale) is how your phone reaches it from outside the house.

Where it shows up in the build

The two phases, mapped

Phase 0 · now

Just the laptop — no networking at all

Everything runs on one machine and talks to itself (localhost). You open Jellyfin and the app in the same laptop's browser. No VPN, no VPS, nothing to reach — the shortest possible network: a machine talking to itself. (That's Primer 02.)

Phase 1 · later

A dedicated home PC + Tailscale

Move the library and Jellyfin to an always-on PC at home, and lay a Tailscale VPN over your devices. Now your phone streams your library from anywhere — through the private tunnel — while the PC stays invisible to the public internet. (Deep-dive: Primer 05.)

The trade-off, honestly

Home + VPN vs cloud VPS

DimensionHome + VPN (our choice)Cloud VPS (rejected)
Where your library livesYour hardware — privateA rented server — the provider's
CostOne-time hardware; VPN freeMonthly rent, and storage for a music library adds up
Control & privacyTotal — nobody else touches the filesYou trust the host with your whole collection
Uptime when you're awayOnly while your home PC is onAlways on (that's what you rent)
Setup complexityInstall Tailscale on two devices — minutesProvision, secure, and maintain a server

The one real thing a VPS buys you — always-on when your home machine is off — isn't worth handing your entire music library to a rented box in the cloud. For a personal library, home + VPN wins on every axis that matters here.

The subtlety worth nailing

“But reaching it from my phone — isn't that the cloud?”

No — and this is the crux. “The cloud” means your data sits on someone else's computer. A VPN moves no data anywhere; it just builds a private, encrypted road between machines you already own. Your music never leaves your house. When you stream from your phone at a café, the files are still at home — you're reaching back to them through the tunnel, not pulling them from a server in the sky.
